Scientific Methodology & Carbon Logic

I. The Biomass Equation

We use the Jenkins Allometric Power Law to estimate total tree weight. Because we can't put a living tree on a scale, we use its diameter (DBH) to predict its mass.

Mass = exp(Constant_A + Constant_B * ln(DBH))

This result gives us the Total Aboveground Biomass in kilograms.

II. Carbon Fraction

Approximately 50% of a tree's dry mass is pure Carbon. This Carbon was physically pulled out of the air as $CO_2$ during photosynthesis to build the wood, bark, and leaves.

III. Atmosphere to Wood ($CO_2$ vs $C$)

To find the amount of $CO_2$ removed from the sky, we multiply the Carbon weight by 3.666. This represents the molecular ratio of Carbon Dioxide to Carbon ($44/12$).

IV. Economic Valuation

We value sequestered carbon at $0.09 per pound. This is derived from the EPA's Social Cost of Carbon (approx. $190 per metric ton).

Final Formula:

[(Biomass × 0.5) × 3.666] × Health Modifier = Total $CO_2$ Saved

Species ID Guide

Hardwood

Slow-growing, dense wood trees like Oaks, Hickory, and Walnut. Broad leaves.

Soft Hardwood

Faster growing deciduous trees like Maple, Poplar, and Birch.

Evergreen

Pine, Cedar, and Spruce. These maintain needles year-round.

Small Ornate

Dogwoods, Holly, and Crepe Myrtles. Usually under 30ft tall.

Tree Health Criteria

1.0 Excellent: Perfect structure, full canopy, no visible pests or damage.

0.8 Good: Normal vitality, minor cosmetic issues or slight thinness.

0.6 Fair: Notable dead branches, thin canopy, or minor trunk damage.

0.4 Poor: Major structural defects, heavy dieback, or severe disease.

0.25 Dead: No living foliage remaining.
